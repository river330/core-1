DESIGNERS ON DESIGN:

an interview with Erin Cochanco
by Rand Rivera

How has your Foundation studies last year informed your art and your practices?

ONE

I think the biggest lesson I learned from foundation year was it's okay to fail. For example, I always wanted to have my own art account but was always too scared to do it. I named my current one "Make Shit Happen" as a double entendre. On one hand, it means to will something to existence through conscious effort. On the other, it means to literally "make shit" as in ugly, useless pieces. I think discovering and owning this mindset was a result of foundation year, as most teachers put an emphasis on experimentation.

Would you say how you grew up influenced your art? If so, how so?

TWO

I grew up as a black sheep in my family --- they're all business people. No one around me really took the time to listen to me and I think art was a way to make people do that. This has made art-making for me a method of beacon shining. I want to send a signal to the world, letting them know who I am so that I can find people like me.

There's this quote that really resonated with me:

“Because—how do you explain that it is never inspiration that drives you to tell a story, but rather a combination of anger and clarity?"* But something else I learned was even if people see you, it doesn't mean they'll understand you. Sometimes people can't change the lens they view you through.

*Valeria Luiselli, Tell Me How It Ends: An Essay in 40 Questions

How do you go about finding inspiration for projects? Any specific artists?

THREE

Inspiration can come from a variety of things like books, movies, jokes, things I overhear --- anything really. Whenever I encounter something interesting, I just want to make out of it. I think it's my way of giving an opinion. I don't have a specific artist I look up to but I'm currently interested in Jenny Odell and Haruichi Furudate.

What is your design process like?

FOUR

I definitely work better under pressure. It's how I've got things done my whole life but I'm beginning to realize it's not a sustainable method.

As for actual designing, it really depends. Sometimes I instantly know how I want something to look, and other times I need to do a bit of research before I figure out what I'm going to do. When it's the latter, I start by defining the goal of the project --- what do I want to achieve, who is this for? Once I get it down in words I proceed to match the concept/s with relevant visuals.

Then comes the fun part. I like to sit in a place where I'll be undisturbed and work for long stretches of time. Illustrator is my go-to software but I'll use Photoshop when the need arises.

How do you push through art blocks and difficulties when it comes to design?

FIVE

I don't push through art blocks, I usually just wait around until I get a lightbulb moment. Personally, I don't agree that discipline pushes through art blocks. I think it just forces you to produce an output so you can say you made something.

It makes me think that art blocks are signs that you're not trying hard enough.

I see art blocks as signs that you've worked your creative muscles too much.

What is your favorite project you've designed and why?

SIX

My senior thesis project, "Chino Ka". In hindsight there were a lot of things that could've been done better but I learned a lot from it. It was a medium to explore questions I've always asked myself, and asking them in public allowed me to meet people with the same curiosities. A lot of Chinese Filipino in my school approached me and my partner to talk about their experiences growing up Chinese in the Philippines. It was probably the project that made me think I really wanted to go for communication design.

What areas of work would you like to explore more that you haven't yet done so?

SEVEN

I kinda want to get into writing. I don't think I'm a very good one so I'd like to improve haha.

What about Communication Design appealed to you as a major?

EIGHT

My previous answers answer this one. In summary, it's because I have a lot of things I want to say, but need to learn how to accurately convey my message.

What is your dream job?

NINE

I don't have a 'dream job', really. I don't have a company or position in mind. I just know I want to make a difference in some small way.

How can design be a tool for social justice?

TEN

Design can be broadly used for social justice. I currently volunteer as a creative for two organizations in the Philippines -- The Lakambini and The Taft Initiative. The Lakambini is a female-centered organization and I create graphics for their Instagram. Right now, we're working on encouraging people to register to vote as the 2022 elections are coming up.

The Taft Initiative is an organization focusing on helping restaurants in the Taft area (a university area so when schools closed they lost most of their customers)
